About

We’re a technology company called Canary. We’re dedicated to preventing workplace harassment and building a safer world for us all.
Filing a claim of sexual harassment can be overwhelming. What should you say? Who should you tell? We are here to help!
This tool will help you write down your experiences, and help you understand your options. It is completely anonymous, and we never save any of your information.


Here's what we can help with:

    Creating clear, timestamped documentation of an experience that you had
    Providing a safe space for you to reflect on your experience
    Describing some of the processes that you may deal with

Here's what we can't help with:

    Giving legal advice
    Escalating issues to your company
    Counseling (though we can point you to some good resources)

Reporting: How-To

In your own company

Each company has their own policies for sexual harassment. These policies should be clearly elaborated in an employee handbook. If you don’t know your employer’s policies or how to raise a concern within your organisation, we recommend you first check the handbook. Then, you can ask your HR representative to explain the process to you.

If you do know the policies and would like to submit a formal grievance letter, Citizens Advice has an online web form to help you build your letter.

    What is an employment tribunal?
    It’s an independent tribunal responsible for hearing claims from people who think someone such as an employer or potential employer has treated them unlawfully.

    What is early conciliation?
    It’s a process where 2 sides of a legal dispute try to reach an agreement before the case is heard in a court or tribunal. This is typically mediated by ACAS - an impartial government-funded organisation dedicated to resolving workplace disputes.
    Make sure to start this process within 3 months less 1 day from the incident in question.
    first
  • You should get advice from a local solicitor or you nearest Citizens Advice before entering a negotiation like this. Your employer may try to settle the dispute with you outside the courts and employment tribunals. An employer only has 2 legal options to do this:

      Negotiate a settlement agreement with you
      This is a legal contract where your employer might promise to pay you a sum of money, stop treating you unlawfully or both.
      Your rights: If you sign a settlement agreement without getting legal advice first, you can still go to an employment tribunal. For this reason, your employer will usually pay for you to get independent legal advice for this negotiation.
      Check if you can get help with legal costs HERE.
      Negotiate a COT3 agreement through ACAS
      These have the same effect as a settlement agreement, but can only be achieved through ACAS.
      Key differences with a settlement agreement:
      1. Usually much simpler format - ACAS has standard forms
      2. Can be entered into verbally, provided ACAS is involved in this process
      3. Relate only to the particular concerns you raised; whereas settlement agreements are typically drafted by the employers’ lawyers so they will seek to settle any and all employment claims as well as put far more obligations on you, the employee
      4. Usually signed by legal representatives; whereas settlement agreements are signed by employees themselves.

    • Employment Tribunal Process Overview

    If you’re intending to make an employment tribunal claim, you need to tell Acas first at www.acas.org.uk/earlyconciliation They will reach out to you and offer Early Conciliation (time limit to make employment tribunal claim pauses during this process) You decide whether or not to participate!
    If yes, Acas will reach out to your employer and mediate the negotiation
    If not, you will need a certificate from Acas to file an employment tribunal claim
    Now that you have your certificate from Acas you can file a claim with the employment tribunal. This is called an ET1 claim. You can find the link to the online form here: https://www.gov.uk/employment-tribunals/make-a-claim) Things to remember when filling out the form:
    This is the first document the tribunal will read if the case goes to a hearing
    Give a clear account of what your claim is about. Keep it simple, but mention every key event
    Write in numbered paragraphs
    List events in the same order that they happened
    Don’t worry about using legal language or quoting the law
    Make sure your complaint is covered by the law
    Finally, submit your form

    In England and Wales You can find out where to send to in England and Wales here:
      http://www.justice.gov.uk/guidance/courts-and-tribunals/tribunals/employment/claims/where-to-send.htm
    In Scotland:
      Central Office of Employment Tribunals (Scotland)
      The Eagle Building
      215 Bothwell Street
      Glasgow
      G2 7TS
      Tel: 0141 204 0730
      Fax: 0141 204 0732
    If you have a representative, they normally send off the form If you have an adviser, make sure you’ve agreed on who’s responsible for sending the form to the tribunal You should receive a Notice of Acknowledgement soon after you send in your ET1 Make sure to check that the tribunal has received the form before the time limit runs out

    The employer’s response to your claim is called an ET3. You should receive this about 4 weeks after you put in your ET1 Simple cases can have a hearing date between 4-6 months after your ET1 form is received More complex cases (e.g. discrimination) may take longer

    If your case is approved, you can go forward with a hearing. Your advisor will be able to guide you through the process
      Timeline to submit an employment tribunal claim: 3 months from the date of the incident less 1 day
      You should look for an adviser/representative to help you, if possible
      Your adviser or representative can help you work out when your time limit starts from. Or you can call one of the following helplines:
      Acas helpline - 0300 123 1100
      Equality Advisory Support Service (EASS) helpline for discrimination cases
      ET1 form must be received at the tribunal office, not just be posted!
      No automatic costs to filing a claim, nor do you automatically have to pay your employer’s legal costs if you lose the case
      Costs orders (one party required to pay another’s legal costs) are only made/approved in less than 1% of cases
      You can withdraw your claim by simply writing to the tribunal saying that you want to withdraw your claim
      If you wait until your employer has spent a lot of money on your claim, you may be at risk of paying costs
